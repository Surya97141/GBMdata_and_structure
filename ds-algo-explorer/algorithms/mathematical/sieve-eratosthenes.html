Sieve of Eratosthenes

Find all primes up to N by iteratively marking composites — an ancient algorithm with modern relevance.

Imagine writing all numbers from 2 to N on a grid. You circle 2 (it's prime), then cross out every 2nd number after it — they can't be prime. You circle the next uncrossed number (3), cross out multiples of 3, and repeat. What remains uncrossed are exactly the primes.

The sieve works because every composite number has a prime factor no larger than its square root. So we only need to start marking from p² (numbers below p² were already marked by smaller primes), and we stop when p > √N. This gives O(N log log N) time — remarkably fast.

Used in cryptography for generating prime tables, in number theory research, and as a preprocessing step for algorithms that need fast prime factorization up to a bound.
