Fermat's Little Theorem

If p is prime and gcd(a,p)=1, then a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p) — and a^(p-2) is a's modular inverse.

Fermat's Little Theorem (1640) states that if p is prime and a is not divisible by p, then a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p). Think of it as: the multiplicative group modulo a prime p has order p-1, so by Lagrange's theorem, any element raised to p-1 gives the identity.

A powerful corollary: a^(p-2) ≡ a⁻¹ (mod p). Since a × a^(p-2) = a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p), we can compute modular inverses without the extended Euclidean algorithm — just by fast exponentiation. This requires p to be prime.

Fermat's theorem is the basis of Fermat primality tests, ElGamal encryption, and is used in competitive programming to compute modular inverse in O(log p) when the modulus is prime (e.g., mod 10⁹+7).
