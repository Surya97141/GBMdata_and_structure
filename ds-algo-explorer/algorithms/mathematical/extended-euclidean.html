Extended Euclidean Algorithm

Find integers x, y such that ax + by = gcd(a, b) — the foundation of modular inverse and RSA.

Bezout's identity guarantees that for any integers a and b, there exist integers x and y such that ax + by = gcd(a, b). The extended Euclidean algorithm finds these Bezout coefficients by tracking extra "bookkeeping" numbers through the standard Euclidean steps.

Think of it as running Euclidean GCD but recording each division step as a linear combination. When we reach the base case (r = 0), we back-substitute the equations to express gcd as a combination of the original inputs.

Critical application: modular inverse. If gcd(a, m) = 1, then x from the equation ax + my = 1 is exactly a⁻¹ mod m — used in RSA decryption, CRT reconstruction, and elliptic curve cryptography.
