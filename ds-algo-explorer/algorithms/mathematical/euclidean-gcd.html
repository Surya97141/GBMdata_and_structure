Euclidean Algorithm (GCD)

Find the greatest common divisor using repeated division — one of the oldest algorithms in existence.

The Euclidean algorithm finds the GCD of two numbers by repeatedly replacing the larger with the remainder of division. The key insight: GCD(a, b) = GCD(b, a mod b), because any common divisor of a and b also divides a - k·b.

Geometrically, think of fitting the largest square into an a×b rectangle. After removing as many b×b squares as fit, you're left with a b×(a mod b) rectangle — the same problem scaled down. Eventually you're left with a square of side GCD(a,b).

The algorithm runs in O(log min(a,b)) steps — Fibonacci numbers are the worst case, requiring exactly the most divisions. Used everywhere: fraction simplification, RSA key generation, and lattice reduction algorithms.
