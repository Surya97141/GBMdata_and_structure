Fast Fourier Transform

Decompose a signal into its frequency components in O(n log n).

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computes the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) in O(n log n) time instead of the naïve O(n²). The DFT converts a time-domain signal (a list of n samples) into a frequency-domain representation (n complex numbers describing amplitude and phase at each frequency).

The analogy: a musical chord sounds like a single complex wave, but it is actually composed of several pure sine waves at specific frequencies (the individual notes). The FFT "listens" to the chord and identifies exactly which notes are present and how loud each is — decomposing complexity into simplicity.

The divide-and-conquer structure (Cooley-Tukey algorithm): split the input into even-indexed and odd-indexed elements, recursively compute their FFTs, then combine using the butterfly operation. The recurrence T(n) = 2T(n/2) + O(n) yields O(n log n). FFT is foundational in signal processing, audio/image compression (MP3, JPEG), and polynomial multiplication.
