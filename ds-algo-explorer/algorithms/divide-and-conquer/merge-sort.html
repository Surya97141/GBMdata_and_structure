Merge Sort

Divide into halves recursively, then merge sorted halves together.

Merge sort is the quintessential divide-and-conquer algorithm. It recursively splits the array in half until each sub-array has one element (trivially sorted), then merges pairs of sorted sub-arrays back together. The merge step is the workhorse: it compares elements from two sorted halves and interleaves them in order.

Think of sorting a large deck of cards. You split the deck in half repeatedly until you have single cards. Then you pick up two single cards and sort them, then pick up two sorted pairs and merge them into a sorted quad, and so on — always merging two already-sorted piles into one larger sorted pile.

Merge sort guarantees O(n log n) in all cases — best, average, and worst — unlike quicksort. It is a stable sort (equal elements retain their original order) and is the preferred algorithm for sorting linked lists or external data (data too large to fit in RAM) due to its sequential access patterns.
