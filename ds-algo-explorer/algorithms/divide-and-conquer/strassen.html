Strassen's Algorithm

Multiply 2×2 matrices with 7 multiplications instead of 8.

Strassen's algorithm is a divide-and-conquer approach to matrix multiplication. For 2×2 matrices, naïve multiplication requires 8 scalar multiplications and 4 additions. Strassen showed in 1969 that 7 multiplications (P1–P7) suffice — the result matrix entries are computed from these 7 products using only additions and subtractions.

The divide-and-conquer structure: split each n×n matrix into four n/2×n/2 sub-matrices, recursively apply Strassen's 7-multiplication scheme to the sub-matrices, and combine. The recurrence T(n) = 7T(n/2) + O(n²) yields complexity O(n^log₂7) ≈ O(n^2.807), beating the naive O(n³).

Though Strassen is asymptotically faster, the smaller constant factor of naive multiplication and cache effects make Strassen practical only for matrices larger than roughly 100×100. It is, however, theoretically important and spawned a rich line of research into sub-cubic matrix multiplication.
