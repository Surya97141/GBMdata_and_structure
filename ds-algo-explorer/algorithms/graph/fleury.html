Fleury's Algorithm

Trace an Euler circuit by greedily avoiding bridge edges — the Königsberg Bridge solution.

Fleury's algorithm finds an Eulerian circuit (a path that visits every edge exactly once and returns to start) in a graph where all vertices have even degree. The rule is deceptively simple: at each step, prefer to traverse any edge that is NOT a bridge — only use a bridge if it's your only option.

A bridge is an edge whose removal disconnects the graph. Crossing a bridge too early can strand you in a part of the graph you've already used up. By checking for bridges at each step (using DFS), Fleury's avoids getting "trapped". The algorithm is named after the mathematician who proved its correctness.

Think of the classic puzzle "draw this figure without lifting your pen": Fleury's gives you the strategy — at each crossing, don't burn the only bridge to unvisited parts. Its time complexity is O(E²) due to bridge-checking at each step, making Hierholzer's preferred for large graphs.

Time Complexity
O(E²)
