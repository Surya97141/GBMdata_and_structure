Binary Search

Halve the search space at every step — logarithmic elegance.

Binary search works on sorted arrays by repeatedly dividing the search interval in half. It compares the target with the middle element: if equal, done; if the target is smaller, search the left half; if larger, search the right half. Each comparison eliminates half the remaining elements.

The classic analogy is a phone book search. Looking for "Smith" you open to the middle — "M" names. Smith comes after M, so you discard the left half. Open to the middle of the right half — "T" names. Discard the right half. Continue until you land on Smith. A 1000-page book takes at most 10 steps.

Binary search achieves O(log n) time because the search space shrinks exponentially: after k steps, at most n/2^k elements remain. It requires O(1) space in its iterative form. The prerequisite is a sorted collection.
