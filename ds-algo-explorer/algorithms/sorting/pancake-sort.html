Pancake Sort

Sort using only prefix reversals — like flipping a stack of pancakes to get the largest on the bottom.

Imagine a stack of pancakes, each a different size. You have a spatula and can only flip the top k pancakes at once (reversing them). Pancake sort uses only this "prefix flip" operation to sort the stack. It's not the most efficient algorithm, but it has a fascinating theoretical connection: the pancake problem asks for the minimum number of flips needed.

The algorithm works in two phases per element: first flip the largest unsorted element to the top of the unsorted region (one flip), then flip it to its correct position at the bottom of the unsorted region (second flip). Each element needs at most 2 flips to place, giving at most 2(n−1) total flips.

The problem has connections to parallel computing (sorting networks), genome rearrangement biology, and the famous "burned pancake" variant. Bill Gates co-authored a paper on pancake sorting in 1979 before founding Microsoft.

Time
O(n²)
At most 2(n-1) flips
Space
O(1)
In-place reversals
Operations
Flips only
Prefix reversal is the only allowed op
