Selection Sort

Pick the smallest, place it, repeat — selection in action.

Selection sort divides the array into two parts: a sorted section at the left and an unsorted section at the right. On each pass it scans the entire unsorted section to find the minimum element, then swaps it into the first position of the unsorted section, growing the sorted partition by one.

Imagine you have a row of numbered cards face-up. You scan all of them, pick up the card with the lowest number, and place it at the leftmost open slot. Then you scan the remaining cards, pick the lowest again, and place it in the next slot — continuing until all cards are placed.

Selection sort always performs exactly n(n-1)/2 comparisons regardless of input, so it has no best-case advantage. However it makes at most n-1 swaps, which can be valuable when writes are expensive. It is not stable in its classic form, as swapping can change the relative order of equal elements.
