Quick Sort

Pick a pivot, partition around it — one of the fastest practical sorting algorithms.

Quick sort picks a "pivot" element and rearranges the array so that all elements smaller than the pivot come before it, and all elements greater come after it. This partition step places the pivot in its final sorted position. The algorithm then recursively applies the same process to the left and right sub-arrays.

Imagine sorting a bookshelf by picking one book as your pivot and making two piles — everything shorter goes left, everything taller goes right. You then repeat the process for each pile. After enough rounds, every book ends up in its correct spot without you ever looking at the full shelf again.

Quick sort achieves O(n log n) average time and is typically the fastest comparison sort in practice due to excellent cache performance and low overhead. The worst case O(n²) occurs with poorly chosen pivots (e.g., always smallest or largest), which can be mitigated with random pivot selection or median-of-three. It is not stable.
