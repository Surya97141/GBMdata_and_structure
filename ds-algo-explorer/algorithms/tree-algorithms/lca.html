Lowest Common Ancestor

The deepest node in a tree that is an ancestor of two given nodes — a fundamental primitive in tree distance, path queries, and phylogenetics.

The Lowest Common Ancestor (LCA) of two nodes u and v in a rooted tree is the deepest (lowest) node that is an ancestor of both. In a family tree analogy, if you trace the lineage of two cousins up through parents and grandparents, the LCA is their most recent common ancestor — perhaps a shared grandparent, not a more distant great-grandparent.

The naive algorithm traces both paths to the root, then finds the deepest intersection. This runs in O(n) in the worst case. The preprocessing-based approach (Euler tour + RMQ, or binary lifting) answers LCA queries in O(1) or O(log n) after O(n log n) preprocessing — essential when millions of queries are needed on the same tree.

LCA appears in computing tree distances: dist(u,v) = depth[u] + depth[v] - 2·depth[LCA(u,v)]. It's used in network routing, version control merge-base computation, and in phylogenetics to find the closest common evolutionary ancestor of two species.
