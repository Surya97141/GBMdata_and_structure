Boyer-Moore Algorithm

Sub-linear pattern matching in practice — match right-to-left and use two heuristics to skip large chunks of text.

Boyer-Moore is the algorithm most text editors use for "Find". It matches the pattern right-to-left and, on mismatch, applies two heuristics to skip as many text positions as possible: the Bad Character Rule (shift so the bad char aligns with its last occurrence in the pattern) and the Good Suffix Rule (shift so a matching suffix aligns with its next occurrence).

Imagine reading a name badge right-to-left. If the last character doesn't match what you expected, you immediately know you can slide the badge sideways by a large amount. This skipping is why Boyer-Moore is sub-linear in practice — it often reads only a fraction of the text characters.

Used in grep, text editors, antivirus scanners, and database query optimisers. For large alphabets (English text), the bad-character heuristic alone gives near O(n/m) average performance.
