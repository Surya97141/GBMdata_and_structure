Octree

The 3D extension of a quad-tree — divides 3D space into 8 octants recursively for fast spatial indexing of 3D scenes.

An Octree is to 3D space what a quad-tree is to 2D: it recursively subdivides a cube into 8 equal sub-cubes (octants), labeled by the sign of each coordinate relative to the center: +++, ++−, +−+, +−−, −++, −+−, −−+, −−−. Each octant that contains more than a threshold of points is itself subdivided.

Imagine a 3D video game world. The entire scene is one big cube. The octree splits it into 8 sub-cubes. Rooms, buildings, and forests subdivide densely; empty sky remains a single large voxel. When the game engine checks whether a bullet hit anything, it descends only the octants the bullet passes through — skipping everything else. Modern AAA game engines use octrees (or related BVH structures) everywhere.

Below we visualize an octree using a 2D top-down projection (XY plane). Each inserted point is classified into one of 8 octants by comparing its (x,y,z) to the center (0,0,0). The canvas shows the XY cross-section with quadrant divisions. The octant table highlights which octant(s) are occupied.
