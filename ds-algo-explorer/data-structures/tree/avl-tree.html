AVL Tree

A self-balancing BST that keeps height difference between subtrees at most 1.

An AVL tree is like a perfectly balanced mobile — every branch must stay within one unit of height of its sibling, or the entire structure automatically rebalances itself. Named after Adelson-Velsky and Landis (1962), it was the first self-balancing BST.

After each insertion, the tree checks the balance factor at every ancestor (left height minus right height). If any node has a balance factor of +2 or -2, one or two rotations are applied to restore balance. There are four cases: LL, RR, LR, and RL.

AVL trees guarantee O(log n) operations in all cases. They shine in read-heavy workloads where look-up speed is critical, such as in-memory databases and symbol tables in compilers. Each node displays its balance factor on the canvas.
